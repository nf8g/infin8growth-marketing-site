AI Governance

Why Your Business Needs an AI Use Policy
(And It's Not About Compliance)

AI Governance By Marshall Tuten May 2, 2026

If you don't have an AI Use Policy, it's a massive strategy problem.

It doesn't matter how big or small your team is. Your team is using AI right now. Whether you've approved it or not. And the question isn't whether that's good or bad. The question is: do you actually know how it's being used? And what are the risks of not having a process around how, why, and where AI is and isn't used?

According to McKinsey's 2025 Global Survey on AI, 78% of organizations now use AI in at least one business function, up from 55% just a year before. Deloitte reports that worker access to AI tools rose by 50% in 2025 alone. The adoption curve is steep, but governance hasn't kept pace.

I ran an event last week in New Haven with a business litigation attorney on AI and the law. The thing that surprised me most wasn't the legal risks we discussed. It was watching a room full of business owners realize they didn't know what AI tools their teams were using, what data was going into them, or whether any of it aligned with what they thought their company stood for.

That's the real risk.

An AI Use Policy is clear documentation showing your organization is being intentional about AI. The document creates an official paper trail. But the biggest win is the clarity you get from doing the work. And in the process of creating it, you naturally address decisions about strategy, vision, tech stack, and culture that most leadership teams have been avoiding.

What Is an AI Use Policy, Actually?

An AI Use Policy is an alignment document that establishes how your organization approaches AI. When most people hear "AI Use Policy," they picture a 40-page legal document that nobody reads. For some organizations, particularly those in regulated industries or with complex operations, a comprehensive policy may indeed run to many pages. For others, something more concise will suffice. The length depends on your organization's size, complexity, and risk profile.

Regardless of length, every AI Use Policy should answer five fundamental questions:

  1. What AI tools are approved, and which aren't? This includes both enterprise tools and the consumer AI products employees might use independently.
  2. What data can and cannot touch those tools? Customer data, proprietary information, internal communications, financial records: each category needs clear boundaries.
  3. Who is accountable for AI-generated outputs? When AI produces something that goes to a customer or influences a decision, someone needs to own that result.
  4. When is human review required? Certain decisions (hiring, pricing, legal communications, customer-facing content) may need human oversight before AI outputs go live.
  5. How do we disclose AI use to customers? Transparency standards vary by industry and jurisdiction, but every organization needs a position on this.

According to ISACA, a global technology governance association, only 28% of organizations have a formal AI policy. That means 72% of businesses are running on assumptions. No documented standards. No shared understanding. No paper trail showing the organization is being intentional about AI.

The value of creating this document is that it requires you to make decisions you've been deferring. You can't write down "what tools are approved" without first knowing what tools people are actually using. You can't define accountability without having a conversation about who owns AI-generated outputs.

The Shadow AI Problem

The gap between AI adoption and AI governance has a name: shadow AI. It refers to employees using AI tools without organizational approval, oversight, or even awareness from leadership.

The numbers are stark. Gartner research from 2025 found that 69% of organizations suspect or have evidence that employees are using prohibited public GenAI tools. Gartner also predicts that by 2030, more than 40% of enterprises will experience security or compliance incidents linked to unauthorized shadow AI.

Microsoft's research paints a similar picture: 71% of UK employees admitted to using unapproved AI tools at work, with 51% doing so at least once a week.

The financial exposure is real. According to IBM's 2025 Cost of a Data Breach Report, AI-associated data breaches cost organizations more than $650,000 on average. And yet, only 37% of organizations have policies in place to manage or even detect shadow AI.

Gartner forecasts that AI governance spending will reach $492 million in 2026 and surpass $1 billion by 2030. Organizations are waking up to this risk, but many are still behind.

Why Does Creating This Document Matter?

An AI Use Policy surfaces four strategic areas that most businesses avoid discussing directly. When you sit down to create this document, you naturally have to address each one.

Strategy

What are we trying to achieve with AI? Are we using it to save time? Improve quality? Reduce costs? Generate new revenue?

Deloitte's 2026 State of AI report found that organizations use AI primarily for productivity and efficiency (66%), decision-making and data-driven insights (53%), and cost reduction (40%). Only 20% are currently using AI to increase revenue.

Most businesses haven't defined their strategic intent clearly. They're experimenting with tools, but there's no shared understanding of why. A policy makes you define the goal. Once you define it, you can measure whether you're getting there.

Vision

How do we see AI fitting into our company in one year? Three years? Are we building capability in-house, or relying on external tools? What role does AI play in how we serve customers?

PwC's 2026 AI Predictions note that more companies are following AI front-runners by adopting an enterprise-wide strategy, where senior leadership picks the spots for focused AI investments in key workflows. A policy requires you to articulate a direction, even if it's simple, even if it evolves.

Tech Stack

What tools are we actually using? All of them, not just the approved ones.

This is where it gets uncomfortable. When you start mapping what's actually happening, you often find that half your team uses ChatGPT, a quarter uses Claude, some use Perplexity, and nobody's using the same prompts, the same verification standards, or the same understanding of what's appropriate. Are employees using personal accounts or company-approved systems? What's compliant and what isn't?

A policy surfaces these questions. That's visibility you didn't have before.

Culture and Mindset

How does your team approach AI? Fear? Excitement? Skepticism?

According to SHRM, employees hide their AI use due to fears of punishment or skepticism. Some worry they won't receive recognition for their achievements if they attribute them to AI. Others fear that productivity gains will lead to job cuts. Research from Gigster found that 57% of employees hide their AI use at work.

That creates a culture of secrecy. And secrecy creates risk. The last thing you want is your team hiding from you or avoiding the massive potential AI offers to your organisation.

A policy gives you the chance to set the tone: we're an organization that uses AI intentionally. Here's how. Here's why. Here's what's expected.

What Happens Without a Policy?

Without a policy, you get shadow AI at scale. Teams use whatever's in front of them. Data goes into tools leadership never approved. There's no consistency in quality or verification. Customers receive AI-generated content without anyone reviewing it. Compliance exposure accumulates until something breaks.

I've seen companies where half the team uses one tool, a quarter uses another, and nobody's operating under the same standards. Instead of leveraging innovation, they're just drifting without aim.

Here's a quote I keep coming back to, from MHP, a Porsche company that studies AI adoption: "AI adoption fails because of a lack of acceptance, uncertainty, or missing know-how."

That's what a policy addresses. The alignment.

The clearer your policy, the easier it is to onboard new team members, evaluate new tools, and actually measure whether AI is working for your business.

The Legal and Regulatory Landscape

A policy becomes legally binding when it's signed, referenced in contracts, or used for compliance. A policy document that sits in a shared drive unread provides no legal protection.

At Infin8, we've done work in the UK where data protection and laws around AI are more defined than in the US. UK GDPR already applies to AI systems: data minimization, purpose limitation, and human oversight for automated decisions are current requirements, not future rules.

In the US, the regulatory landscape is evolving rapidly. The FTC published its AI Policy Statement in March 2026, establishing the first federal enforcement framework for AI agents, automated decisions, and AI-generated content. Fines up to $53,000 per violation take effect in 2027.

Key compliance requirements from the FTC framework include:

  • Documentation and logging: If your AI makes consumer-affecting decisions, you need records of the criteria, inputs, and outputs.
  • Disclosure requirements: If an AI system interacts with a consumer, they need to know it's AI.
  • Model deletion as enforcement: The FTC can order deletion of models trained on improperly collected data.

State laws add another layer. Connecticut's AI provisions take effect July 1, 2026. Colorado's AI Act becomes effective August 2026. California, Illinois, and Texas all have different rules in development or already active.

The principle to remember: laws follow your customers, not your office. If you serve UK customers, GDPR matters even if you're based in the US. If you serve customers in Connecticut, those state provisions apply regardless of where your headquarters is located.

5 Questions to Answer This Week

If you want to start thinking about what would be in your policy, answer these five questions. If you can answer them clearly, you're on track. If you can't, you've identified your homework.

  1. What AI tools are we using? All of them. Not just the approved ones. Survey your team. You might be surprised by what you find.
  2. What data touches those tools? Customer data? Proprietary code? Internal docs? Financial information? If you don't know, you have a gap.
  3. Who is accountable for outputs? If AI drafts an email with an error, who owns that? If it generates a report with a mistake, who's responsible?
  4. What decisions require human review? Hiring? Pricing? Customer communications? Legal documents? Some things need a human in the loop. Define which ones.
  5. How do we tell customers? When do we disclose that AI was involved? What transparency standards do we hold ourselves to?

Frequently asked questions

What is an AI Use Policy?
An AI Use Policy is an alignment document that answers five questions: what AI tools are approved, what data can touch those tools, who is accountable for outputs, when human review is required, and how AI use is disclosed to customers. The length and complexity depends on your organization. It's documentation of intentionality, providing a paper trail for how you approach AI.
Do small businesses need an AI Use Policy?
Yes, regardless of company size. According to ISACA, only 28% of organizations have a formal AI policy, meaning 72% are running on assumptions. Gartner research shows 69% of organizations suspect employees are using prohibited public AI tools. A policy ensures everyone operates with the same understanding of what's appropriate.
What is shadow AI and why is it a risk?
Shadow AI refers to employees using AI tools without organizational approval or oversight. Gartner predicts 40% of enterprises will experience security or compliance incidents from shadow AI by 2030. Microsoft research found 71% of UK employees admit to using unapproved AI tools at work. IBM reports AI-associated data breaches cost organizations over $650,000 on average.
How long does it take to create an AI Use Policy?
The timeline depends on your organization's size and complexity. A basic policy answering the five core questions can be drafted in a few leadership meetings. Larger organizations may need more extensive documentation covering different departments and use cases. The value comes from the conversations the process requires: defining strategy, mapping tools, and setting expectations.
What are the legal requirements for AI policies in 2026?
In the US, the FTC published its AI Policy Statement in March 2026, with fines up to $53,000 per violation starting in 2027.
